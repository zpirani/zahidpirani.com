Why now

Shell is large enough that strong solutions get built in isolation. Voltron (Financial reporting), ORION (built by the US trading team), and the Terminal Cockpit modules each solved a real problem on their own — but independently, with no shared home. TPC is the decision to converge those silos into one platform rather than let them keep diverging.

The Problem

There's no single, layered view of how Shell's terminals are performing. Financial performance is hard to read across levels. Live operational insight — inventory, ownership where terminal space is leased, products held per terminal, and vessel movements — lives in ORION. Operator tooling lives in the separate Terminal Cockpit modules. Each is useful alone; none of them connect.

My Role

I'm the sole designer, leading discovery. With BAs and engineers not yet onboarded, the early work of making sense of the problem — process flows, personas, primary research, and pattern exploration — has been entirely design-led.

The core design challenge

Five personas operate at very different altitudes, and the three systems converging into TPC each carry their own internal logic. The hard problem: how do you make one coherent platform without flattening it into a one-size-fits-all dashboard that serves nobody?

Three converging pillars

  • Financial performance dashboards — terminal financial performance at global, cluster, and terminal levels.
  • ORION — the US trading team's dashboards: live inventory across terminals, ownership breakdown for leased space, product visibility per terminal, and vessel tracking/movements.
  • Terminal apps — the operator modules from Terminal Cockpit, so terminal teams work inside the same platform.

Research: "A day in the life of a terminal"

To ground the operational personas, I ran a primary research study mapping a control-room operator's full shift — from trading nominations through execution, reconciliation, and handover. The as-is journey surfaced a consistent pattern of manual, paper-and-fax-dependent work: nominations arriving by fax, instructions hand-copied onto a physical whiteboard, rail and tank data re-entered across disconnected systems, and triple reconciliation by hand.

The headline pain points: heavy duplication (the same data rewritten across board, shift report, reconciliation, and email), communication gaps (fax dependency with no guaranteed acknowledgment), system fragmentation (HMI, rail, scheduling, and email all unintegrated), and the operational risk that comes with manual coordination.

The key insight that reframed the project: the operation isn't broken — it's highly reliable, but manual. The opportunity isn't to rip out the process; it's to digitize without losing the physical "signal" operators depend on — the sound of the fax, the board on the wall. That points to a hybrid approach (digital data plus visual, ambient cues) as the path to adoption.

Pillar 2 in detail — ORION (the trading intelligence being absorbed)

Unlike the financial dashboards and the converged operator apps, ORION already exists as built, trader-facing work — designed end-to-end with Shell's US trading team. It's the most mature of the three pillars, and its screens are being brought directly into TPC as the Trader's layer. The context below frames the ORION screens now being imported.

ORION covers two capability areas:

Inventory Intelligence

A commercial view of terminal inventory built on one reframe: traders don't think in tanks, they think in customers. The data architecture was flipped from a technical view (Tank 101 holds X) to a commercial one (Customer A has X capacity left). The hero view is a high-density customer-holdings chart; the ATS (Available to Sell) engine distills contracted-vs-actual, min/max thresholds, and safety heels into a single actionable number; and a 7/30-day trajectory chart shows when a customer will hit the ceiling of their capacity. Designing for data lineage and transparency was essential to win the trust of terminal staff skeptical of digital dashboards.

Vessel Movements

A real-time berth scheduling and tracking system built on a dual-view architecture — a Gantt scheduler for temporal planning and a live data table for operational depth, linked by one shared status-color language (teal berthed, amber inbound, coral delayed, grey departed). Conflict detection is built into the Gantt's visual logic; net-change logic projects inventory levels post-movement; and trust markers (data-freshness indicators, graceful handling of estimated vs confirmed ETAs) carry users through the transition from spreadsheets to live data. The module is explicitly designed for shift handovers.

Why this matters for TPC

ORION's status-color language is consistent with the standardized status treatment from the Terminal Cockpit redesign — so a status reads the same way whether a trader is looking at a vessel in ORION or an operator is looking at an alarm in a terminal app. That shared language is part of what makes convergence into one coherent platform possible rather than three systems sharing a shell.
