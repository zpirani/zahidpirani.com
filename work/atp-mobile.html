Context

Axi is most widely known as a MetaTrader broker. In 2021, the company chose to build its own proprietary trading platform — ATP — to address the parts of the retail trading experience MT4 wasn't solving: clean modern UX, a curated onboarding journey, and a coherent design language across the product ecosystem.

ATP launched mobile-first. For more than two years — from launch through late 2023 — mobile was the only ATP surface. Every interaction model, every design pattern, every visual decision was proven on mobile first. The web platform that came online in late 2023 inherited what mobile had built.

I joined Axi in November 2022, mid-way through that mobile-only period. My scope was the entire mobile product. My first task: mobile onboarding.

The challenge

Three problems sat on top of every mobile design decision:

  1. Make CFD trading accessible without dumbing it down. Active retail traders demand professional-grade tools — advanced charts, order types, real-time data. New traders need an experience that doesn't overwhelm. The same product had to serve both.
  2. Onboard users compliantly across 180+ jurisdictions — a single mobile flow that satisfied FCA (UK), CySEC (Cyprus), ASIC (Australia), DFSA (Dubai), FMA (New Zealand), and many more — including specialised paths like W-8BEN for US residents and high-risk-flow Jumio verification where the regulatory environment required it.
  3. Carry the entire ATP product on mobile for two years. Until web launched in late 2023, mobile was the product. Every surface — from onboarding through execution — had to work on a phone, in one hand, in the moments traders actually had.

Onboarding — the first thing I shipped, the most complex thing I shipped

Mobile onboarding was my first task at Axi and remains the case study's strongest single beat.

The V1 → V2 progression

Where V1 fell short:

  • Huge forms that asked for everything up front
  • Confusing copy dominated by regulatory language
  • Drop-off cliffs at the high-friction steps (document upload, funding readiness)

What V2 changed:

  • Chunked the forms. Long monolithic forms became small focused steps. Each step asked for the minimum needed to progress.
  • Adaptive jurisdiction logic. Conditional content per jurisdiction — US residents got W-8BEN; ASIC users got Australian-residency questions; CySEC users got their own. One flow, many shapes.
  • Jumio inserted conditionally — specifically in the high-risk flow where regulator-mandated identity verification required it. Low-risk users skipped it. This avoided forcing every applicant through camera-capture friction.
  • Rewrote the copy. Compliance-required language was kept legally intact but reframed in a warmer voice.
  • Progress stepper above every step, so users always knew how close they were to done.

Measured impact (mobile cohort via Amplitude)

  • −35% in account setup time
  • 2× conversion uplift across Direct, Affiliate, and IB acquisition channels

The flow itself ran consistently across mobile and later web; mobile is the cohort I have clean data for. The interview-defensible framing: "We designed for both surfaces. Mobile launched first and was where we measured impact via Amplitude."
